## Abstract

How should we price an asset class that has no historical transaction data — not because it is novel, but because the institutional constraints that *define* it as an asset have only recently been established? This paper develops a formal constraint-based asset pricing framework that derives value from the structure of institutional constraints rather than from historical cash flow data. Building on Barzel's insight that assets are bundles of enforceable property rights, we formalize each constraint as an institutional triple (Rule, Enforcement, Information) and define its completeness η ∈ [0,1]. We introduce a multi-layer constraint architecture, show that inter-layer coupling produces a snowball amplification effect (Λ = ∏φ_{ij}), and derive an explicit pricing function g that maps constraint structure to asset value. The framework is applied to two emerging asset classes — low-altitude airspace and data access rights — and a calibration roadmap is provided. The framework demonstrates that asset pricing in the absence of historical data is not only possible but analytically tractable when the constraint structure is known.

## 1. Introduction

Traditional asset pricing theory — from discounted cash flow (DCF) to the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to arbitrage-free pricing — rests on a foundational assumption: the asset already exists, and its value can be discovered from market signals. Expected cash flows are estimated from historical data. Discount rates are calibrated from observed risk premia. The pricing exercise is one of *measurement*.

But what if the asset does not yet exist — not in a physical sense, but in an institutional sense? What if the constraints that define it as a tradable, excludable, enforceable asset have only recently been established, leaving no historical transaction data, no claims history, no observable risk premia?

This is not a hypothetical scenario. It describes the current state of two emerging asset classes of significant policy and economic interest: low-altitude airspace rights (the right to operate unmanned aerial vehicles in designated corridors) and data access rights (the right to access, use, and commercialize specific data assets under defined conditions). Both are being actively "assetized" — transformed from non-excludable commons into tradable assets — through the construction of institutional constraint frameworks. Yet traditional pricing methods fail at the very moment they are most needed: when the asset is born.

This paper develops a constraint-based asset pricing framework that addresses this structural gap. The core insight is simple: **the value of an infrastructure-defined asset is a function of its constraint structure, not a discovery of pre-existing value.** The framework is built on three theoretical foundations:

First, **Barzel's (1997) property rights economics**: assets are not physical objects but bundles of enforceable rights.

Second, **Coase (1960) and Williamson (1985) on transaction costs and governance**: the institutional form of constraints determines their effectiveness and cost.

Third, **Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC)**: constraints are not merely obstacles but the defining structure of any system's productive capacity.

To these we add a formal architecture: each constraint is modeled as an institutional triple C = (R, ε, I) — Rule, Enforcement, Information. Constraint completeness η(C) ∈ [0,1] measures the effective enforcement of a constraint. Multiple constraints operate in layers (physical, digital compliance, financial, governance), and the interaction between layers — captured by a coupling matrix Φ — produces snowball amplification effects where joint failure probability exceeds the product of independent failure probabilities.

The result is a pricing function g that maps constraint structure to asset value:

```
V = N × Cred × H × |Ω_eff⁺| × P̄ × q̄ / (1 + r_f + RP_constraint)
```

The contribution of this paper is threefold. First, it provides a **formal framework** for pricing assets defined by institutional constraints rather than physical properties, filling the gap between property rights theory and quantitative asset pricing. Second, it introduces the **snowball effect** — the formal amplification of risk when multiple constraint layers fail jointly — as a previously unidentified source of systematic risk. Third, it demonstrates the framework's applicability to two emerging asset classes of significant policy relevance.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 develops the constraint theory of asset value. Section 3 presents the multi-layer constraint architecture and the snowball effect. Section 4 derives the pricing function. Section 5 applies the framework to low-altitude airspace and data access rights. Section 6 outlines the empirical calibration strategy. Section 7 concludes.

## 2. The Constraint Theory of Asset Value

### 2.1 Assets as Bundles of Enforceable Constraints

Barzel (1997) defined an asset as a bundle of enforceable property rights. The economic value of a physical object derives not from its material properties but from the institutional constraints that define who can access it, under what conditions, and at what cost. A tree in an unowned forest has no asset value; the same tree, when enclosed by "unauthorized logging is prohibited and punishable," acquires asset value. The constraint creates scarcity, and scarcity enables pricing.

This insight has a philosophical foundation that predates modern economics. Hegel's *Science of Logic* articulated the principle that "determination is negation" (*Bestimmung ist Negation*) — any affirmative determination of what something *is* simultaneously declares what it is *not*. A constraint, in economic terms, is the institutional embodiment of this negation: it declares what is *not permitted*, and in doing so, defines what *is* permitted.

### 2.2 The Constraint Triple: Rule, Enforcement, Information

A constraint, to be economically meaningful, must be more than a written rule. The Suzhou R44 helicopter crash of 2025 illustrates why. Digital compliance constraints existed on paper: flight approvals were required, and operational limits were specified. But the enforcement mechanism was absent — the UOM system did not automatically lock out flights that exceeded approved parameters. The rule existed, but enforcement was effectively zero. The constraint was a paper tiger.

We formalize each constraint C as an institutional triple:

```
C = (R, ε, I)
```

where:

- **R: Ω → {0,1}** is the Rule predicate. R(ω) = 1 if the state satisfies the constraint.
- **ε: Ω⁻ → [0,1] × ℝ⁺** is the Enforcement function. ε(ω) = (p_detect(ω), s_penalty(ω)).
- **I ⊆ Ω** is the Information basis — the subset of the state space that the enforcement mechanism can observe.

The **constraint completeness** η(C) ∈ [0,1] measures the effective enforcement:

```
η(C) = E_{ω∈Ω⁻}[p_detect(ω)] × (|Ω⁻ ∩ I| / |Ω⁻|)
```

### 2.3 The Embodiment Gap: Why η < 1 is Principled

η(C) is always strictly less than 1 for any institutional constraint. This is not a failure of institutional design; it is a principled limit arising from the gap between abstract negation and operational rules. The Rule component R must specify, in finite operational language, what is prohibited. But the underlying negation — the abstract determination of the asset's boundary — is infinite in its exclusion. This conceptual granularity gap is irreducible.

We term this structural loss the **Embodiment Gap**: the necessary discount between the abstract negation that defines the asset's boundary and the institutional constraint that enforces it. The effective value of an asset is its value under perfect constraints multiplied by the multi-layer completeness discount H, where H ∈ [min_i η_i, ∏_i η_i].

## 3. Multi-Layer Constraint Architecture

### 3.1 The Four-Layer Framework

Constraints do not operate in isolation. They are organized in layers, each with a distinct enforcement mechanism type and information basis type:

| Layer | Type | Core Question | Enforcement Mechanism |
|-------|------|---------------|----------------------|
| L₁ | Physical | Can it fly? | Laws of physics |
| L₂ | Digital Compliance | Can it fly legally? | Automated system interception |
| L₃ | Financial | Can it be priced and traded? | Market access, premiums, interest rates |
| L₄ | Governance | Is it integrated into the socio-economic system? | State enforcement power |

A state must satisfy the constraints of *all* layers to be executable. If any layer's feasible set is empty, the global feasible set is empty — the asset does not exist.

### 3.2 Inter-Layer Coupling and the Snowball Effect

Constraint layers are not independent. When one layer fails, it increases the conditional probability that other layers fail as well. We define the **coupling coefficient**:

```
φ_{ij} = P(Layer i fails | Layer j fails) / P(Layer i fails)
```

where φ_{ij} = 1 indicates independence, and φ_{ij} > 1 indicates positive coupling. The coupling structure is captured by the **coupling matrix** Φ = [φ_{ij}].

The **snowball amplification factor** Λ is:

```
Λ = P_cascade / P_independent = ∏_{(i,j)∈cascade_path} φ_{ij}
```

A critical special case arises when any layer has p_i = 1 (certain absence). In this case, Λ transforms from a probability amplifier into a **deterministic fuse**: certainty propagates through the coupling chain, converting probabilistic risk into certain disaster. The 2016 Jinshan seaplane crash — where L₂ (digital compliance) and L₃ (financial) were both deterministically absent — exemplifies this case.

## 4. The Pricing Function

### 4.1 From Constraint Structure to Effective State Space

The first step of constraint-based pricing maps the constraint structure to an effective state space:

```
(C_set, Φ, H) → Ω_eff⁺
```

where Ω_eff⁺ = Ω_global⁺ × H is the effective feasible state space corrected for the embodiment gap. If the negation channel does not exist (N = 0) or channel credibility is zero (Cred = 0), Ω_eff⁺ = ∅ and the asset has no value.

### 4.2 The g Function

```
V = N × Cred × H × |Ω_global⁺| × P̄_D × q̄ / (1 + r_f + RP_constraint)
```

where N ∈ {0,1} is negation channel existence, Cred ∈ [0,1] is channel credibility (Cred = 1 - Λ × ∏_i p_i), H ∈ [0,1] is the embodiment gap discount, P̄_D is willingness to pay, q̄ is demand volume, r_f is the risk-free rate, and RP_constraint is the constraint risk premium.

### 4.3 Constraint Risk Premium

```
RP_constraint = α₁ × Λ × ∏_i p_i  +  α₂ × σ_Cred  +  α₃ × σ_η
```

The three components capture snowball risk (Λ × ∏p_i), credibility volatility (σ_Cred), and embodiment drift (σ_η).

An important feature: constraint risk is potentially **orthogonal to traditional financial risk factors**. Snowball risk is driven by institutional-political cycles; credibility volatility by reflexive belief dynamics; embodiment drift by technological decay. None follow equity market cycles directly.

### 4.4 Relationship to Traditional DCF

The g function is formally compatible with DCF: V = Σ_t [E_CF(Ω_eff⁺, D_t)] / (1 + r_t)^t, where expected cash flows are derived from the effective state space rather than historical data. Distinctiveness lies in the **derivation chain** of inputs — from constraint structure rather than from historical statistics.

## 5. Applications

### 5.1 Low-Altitude Airspace as an Asset Class

Low-altitude airspace is being actively transformed from an unmanaged commons into a priceable asset class through UOM systems, airspace classification standards, and aviation insurance development.

The constraint structure is nascent. L₁ (physical) and L₂ (digital compliance) are partially in place. L₃ (financial) is almost entirely absent — there is virtually no low-altitude aviation insurance market. L₄ (governance) is latent.

The framework identifies L₃ as the critical missing layer. Its absence removes the incentive anchor that would otherwise compel operators to comply with L₂. The Suzhou crash of 2025 is the canonical illustration: L₃ absence → operators have no cost incentive to comply → L₂ loopholes are exploited → engine failure → pedestrian fatality.

### 5.2 Data Access Rights as an Asset Class

Data assets present a distinct structural profile: their negation must be entirely institutionally constructed, since data is infinitely replicable at near-zero marginal cost.

Current approaches predominantly follow a "token pricing" model — treating data as a commodity priced by volume. This fails for three structural reasons: it cannot price the same data differently for different users, cannot enforce post-sale usage constraints, and produces price signals carrying no constraint information.

Constraint-based pricing reframes the question: not "what is this dataset worth?" but "what is the access right — for a specific user, under specific conditions, for a specific purpose, for a specific duration — worth?"

### 5.3 From Land Finance to Airspace/Data RMB

A macro-implication concerns China's transition from land-based to infrastructure-based credit creation. Land served as the anchor asset for RMB credit expansion for three decades. This cycle is now reaching its limits.

The framework identifies airspace and data as two parallel candidate anchors for RMB credit migration. Both satisfy the structural conditions: definability, enforceability, and priceability. A dual-track migration — data on the fast track (1-3 years), airspace on the steady track (3-5 years) — is the recommended path.

## 6. Empirical Calibration Strategy

Parameters fall into three calibration tiers:

**Tier 1 (Market-observable)**: P̄_D, q̄, r_f.

**Tier 2 (Audit-estimable)**: η_i, p_i, |Ω_global⁺|.

**Tier 3 (Calibration-dependent)**: φ_{ij}, α_k — require tradable constraint-risk assets which do not yet exist.

A four-stage calibration roadmap:

- **Stage 1 (Current)**: Placeholder parameters with expert-judgment intervals. Output is a rate range.
- **Stage 2 (After first insurance policies)**: Bayesian updating of p_i and η_i from claim data.
- **Stage 3 (After design institute endorsement)**: Calibrate capacity and market parameters from feasibility studies.
- **Stage 4 (After reinsurance recognition)**: Back-estimate α_k from reinsurance pricing.

The empirical validation infrastructure is a proprietary flight data platform recording multi-dimensional spatiotemporal data for each flight: time, space, operator, equipment, and scene. Each flight record contributes to a cumulative risk matrix from which η_i, φ_{ij}, and Λ can be progressively estimated.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper has developed a constraint-based asset pricing framework addressing a structural gap: how to price assets with no historical transaction or claims data. Five contributions:

1. **Formalization of constraint as an institutional triple** C = (R, ε, I), with η(C) ∈ [0,1].

2. **Identification of the snowball effect** as a distinct source of systematic risk.

3. **Derivation of an explicit pricing function g** mapping constraint structure to asset value.

4. **Application to two emerging asset classes**: low-altitude airspace rights and data access rights.

5. **Identification of constraint risk as a potentially diversifying risk factor** orthogonal to traditional financial factors.

The framework is at an early stage of empirical calibration. The path to validation runs through the cumulative risk matrix of operational flight data. The ultimate test is predictive validity: can the constraint structure of an airspace corridor predict its claim rate?
